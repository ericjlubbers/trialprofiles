Carlos A. Samour Jr.

Title: Chief Judge, 18th Judicial District

Admitted to the bar: Oct. 1990

Salary: $145,219

Prior cases: Oversaw the case of a driver convicted of two road-rage deaths on E-470. Appointed a special prosecutor to pursue a rape case against two men after the Arapahoe County district attorney's office declined to file charges, a decision later overturned by an appellate court.

George Brauchler

Title: 18th Judicial District Attorney

Admitted to the bar: Oct. 1995

Salary: $156,000

Prior cases: While working as a military lawyer, prosecuted the case of a Fort Carson soldier who murdered a Taliban commander in an Afghan jail cell. Blasted a decision by Gov. John Hickenlooper to grant a reprieve from execution to convicted killer Nathan Dunlap, a protest that drew considerable attention and prompted Brauchler to consider a run for governor.

Karen Pearson

Title: Chief Deputy District Attorney

Admitted to the bar: Oct. 1995

Salary: $120,000

Prior cases: A veteran prosecutor of numerous high-profile felony cases, she handled the case of a driver who killed three people in a crash into an ice cream parlor, a case during which she was accused of undermining a police detective. Also prosecuted the case of former Arapahoe County Sheriff Patrick Sullivan.

Name: Rich Orman

Title: Senior Deputy District Attorney

Admitted to the bar: Oct. 1991

Salary: $107,000

Prior cases: Prosecuted a former CIA contractor accused in a parking lot assault, as well as being part of the team that prosecuted a driver who killed three when he crashed into an ice cream parlor.

Name: Jacob Edson

Title: Chief Deputy District Attorney

Admitted to the bar: May 2005

Salary: $109,000

Prior cases: Prosecuted a man accused of killing his ex-girlfriend in 2007. Chief of the DA's office of economic crimes.

Name: Lisa Teesch-Maguire

Title: Deputy District Attorney

Admitted to the bar: Oct. 2004

Salary: $90,614*

Prior cases: Hired specially to handle victim's rights issues, after prior work with the Rocky Mountain Victim Law Center. Previously worked as an assistant attorney general in American Samoa, a post she left after receiving death threats over her successful prosecution of a rape case.

*Teesch-Maguire's salary was previously covered by a federal victim-assistance grant. That grant does not cover prosecution expenses, though, so the district attorney's office will pay her salary for 2015.

Jury

A look at what we know about the 19 women and five men who will serve as jurors in the James Holmes trial.

  • Seat 1: Juror No. 640. Works as a plumber. Said she has been diagnosed with ADHD. "I know what it's like to be a little bit different from other people," she said during individual questioning.

  • Seat 2: No. 17. Lawyer and business owner. Of the death penalty, she said: "I think it has to be the exception to the rule, rather than the rule."

  • Seat 3: No. 329

  • Seat 4: No. 661 Teaches classes on dental assisting. Daughter attended premiere of the Batman movie on the same night as the shooting but at a different theater. Defense tried to get her removed from the pool.

  • Seat 5: No. 535. "I don't think I'd have a problem at all with guilty, not guilty, not guilty by reason of insanity," she said during group questioning.

  • Seat 6: No. 87. On her juror questionnaire, she wrote, "I used to believe in eye-for-an-eye, but now, knowing more about mental illness, I would have to consider everything involved."

  • Seat 7: No. 901. Interviewed in February for a position with the FBI that she did not receive. Dismissed on Day 31 in connection with an incident involving her brother-in-law being shot in an attempted robbery.

  • Seat 8: No. 495. Teared up during individual questioning when asked whether she could deliver a death sentence. Dismissed, along with two other jurors, on Day 28 stemming from an incident of media exposure.

  • Seat 9: No. 872. Dismissed, along with two other jurors, on Day 28 of the trial after it was brought to light that her husband had talked about the case on speakerphone during a break in the trial and did not report it to the judge.

  • Seat 10: No. 1,009. Said during group questioning that she didn't have many concerns about serving on the jury. "My main concern was just my privacy," she said.

  • Seat 11: No. 118. Said her son-in-law is a corrections officer and veteran who suffers from PTSD. "I think mental illness is a fact of life," she said. "A lot of us have been touched by it."

  • Seat 12: No. 983.

  • Seat 13: No. 378. Expressed reservations about how the outcome of the case will be perceived by the public. "Of course, it's going to be in the back of my mind," she said. "But it's going to have to stay in the back."

  • Seat 14: No. 155. Was living in California at the time of the shooting. He didn't know how many people were killed and wounded in the shooting until he came to court.

  • Seat 15: No. 527.

  • Seat 16: No. 673. Woman who serves in the military. Has three kids, and her husband is deployed. Asked whether she wanted to serve on the jury, she said, "I don't want to be part of it, to be honest."

  • Seat 17: No. 737. "I'm the world's worst liar," he said during group questioning. In the early stages of the trial he told judge he developed a "new friendship" with one survivor, but was allowed to remain on the jury.

  • Seat 18: No. 706. Retired after a 22-year career in the Army as an intensive care nurse. Asked whether she could handle weighing the numerous charges in the case, she replied, "How do you eat an elephant? One bite at a time."

  • Seat 19: No. 412. Works in the information technology department at the Aurora Mental Health Center. Previously served as a law enforcement agent in the Air Force and called herself "very strongly in the middle." Dismissed, along with two other jurors, on Day 28 stemming from an incident of media exposure.

  • Seat 20: No. 267. Works as an attendance clerk at a school. Two students from her school were at the Aurora theater that night, she said. Juror 267 was dismissed on Day 33 in relation to her recognition of a witness on May 6, 2015.

  • Seat 21: No. 557. Said she found the prospect of serving on the jury scary. "But it's part of our responsibility and our legal system," she said.

  • Seat 22: No. 307. Works as a teacher of special-education children. Said she used to be more liberal and against the death penalty but has become more open to it. "Just growing up," she said.

  • Seat 23: No. 311. Said she needed to hear all the evidence in the case before she could make a decision. "I don't think you actually know what you'd really do until you hear everything."

  • Seat 24: No. 313. Works at a gas station. His employer won't pay him while he's serving, but he said he could scrape by on $50 a day.
